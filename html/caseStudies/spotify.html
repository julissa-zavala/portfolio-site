Spotify: New feature addition


Role
UX/UI Designer
Researcher
Project Duration
2 weeks (40 hrs/wk)
UX Academy Student Project
tools
Sketch, Miro, Invision, Figma, Maze

Overview


As the leading music streaming platform, Spotify wants to maintain their position as top dog by expanding their social capabilities. Their goal is to improve social engagement on their platform as well as user retention rates.


The Problem

Within the past few years, Spotify has tapped into music lovers’ proclivity for sharing their music taste with others with Spotify Wrapped. This popular year-end review of users’ favorite artists, songs, genres, and podcasts becomes a trending social media conversation every December.

While Spotify has other social features that foster user-to-user interaction, like following artists or friends and an activity feed sidebar, nothing has made quite an impact as Spotify Wrapped has.

How might we add a social feature to Spotify that eventually garners the amount of user engagement Spotify Wrapped gets?

The Solution

  • A way for users to look back at their listening habits, similar to Spotify Wrapped, but with a twist--users can see their listening habits in relation to other users and receive a music compatibility score.
  • Users can use this feature to learn more about their friends’ and acquaintances’ music tastes.
  • Users will receive a curated shared playlist with the user they’re comparing their music compatibility with.
  • Users can also use this feature to discover new music.

Design Thinking Process

* * * * *

Research


Hypothesis

Based on how popular Spotify Wrapped is, there seems to be a desire by users to share their music taste with others, and get a review of what they listened to. With that desire in mind, I believe that adding a music compatibility feature on user’s profiles will meet that desire in a way that no other music streaming site is currently doing.

My inspiration for this feature is a music compatibility feature that Last.Fm has. Though Last.Fm is not a music streaming site, its ability to track what you listen to in order to connect with others with similar music taste is an understated feature with tremendous potential.

Research Goals and Objectives

My main research goal is to test my hypothesis on whether or not a music compatibility feature will be successful in creating a more social and interactive experience for Spotify users.

My secondary research goal is to develop an in-depth understanding on what users currently enjoy about Spotify compared to its competitors, and what users think the app lacks.

My research objectives were:

  • What are the social capabilities that exist across different music streaming sites?
  • What pain points currently exist within the Spotify experience?
  • What makes using Spotify an enjoyable experience?
  • Understand how users currently interact with other users on Spotify.
  • Discover if users would benefit from a music compatibility feature.

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive analysis to determine what popular music streaming companies are doing to create a social element to their app’s experience and see if there is any overlap as well as opportunities for disruption.



While Last.FM is not a direct competitor, I wanted to delve into what their site offered as a music social networking site and compare those features with the social features Spotify, Apple Music, and Tidal offer.

My research showed that Apple Music is curreently Spotify’s biggest competitor. While Spotify offers more social features than Apple Music with group listening sessions and collaborative playlists, Apple Music has a stronger user profile interface. For example, it’s much easier to find other user profiles on Apple Music than on Spotify.


Secondary Research

I explored Last.fm's music compatibility feature and identified its strengths and weaknesses.



Then, I explored forums and communities where Last.fm users created more accurate compatibility tools as well as their own data visualizations for this feature.

Based on my research on these user-created tools there appears to be a desire amongst users to:

  • Have a more accurate compatibility feature with a filter to pick your own timespan on the data pulled.
  • See more transparency on the algorithms used to calculate music compatibility.
  • See more than three overlapping artists between users.
  • See this data visualized in a more aesthetically pleasing way.

My research also demonstrates that Last.fm appeals to ‘die hard’ music lovers who enjoy looking at data and statistics regarding their music listening habits. While the above desires and needs of this user base are useful to know, they might not meet the desires and needs of the average Spotify user.

By conducting user interviews with Spotify users, I can find out what a Spotify user would want from a music compatibility feature and see if there is any overlap between Spotify and Last.fm users.

User Interviews

I conducted user interviews with 3 participants to gain an understanding of how a music compatibility feature can foster social interaction and whether or not it is needed.

I recruited participants who I considered to be ‘Spotify Power Users.’ These are Spotify users that frequently use Spotify and follow at least 30 users on Spotify.

Some notable findings regarding thoughts on music compatibility in general and a potential music comptability feature:

  • All three participants said finding people with similar music taste as them is important.
  • 2 out of the 3 participants said they would want to see their music compatibility with people they don’t know that well
  • 2 out of 3 participants said this feature would change the way they use Spotify
  • All 3 participants said this feature would make them visit other user’s profiles more often
" “I think that this feature would make Spotify more of a social app. Right now, the activity feed is a pretty passive feature. It’s not a main feature, it just catches my eye on the side.”"



When asked what an ideal music compatibility feature would look like, participants said:

  • They would like to see overlapping artists between themselves and another user.
    1 participant said that if it’s a lesser known artist, just seeing that her and another user listen to that artist would suffice. However, if the artist is someone with a bigger name or a more prolific body of work, she would want it to say what specific album or song you both listened to.
  • They would like to see random fun facts like the ones Spotify Wrapped provides. These include facts like:
    1. "You both listened to [x] album a lot the year it came out"
    2. "This user listened to this album 50 times."

Branding Research

Since my goal is to develop a music compatibility feature and integrate that feature into Spotify’s existing design system, it’s important to take a look at Spotify’s biggest example of data visualization found on the app: Spotify Wrapped.




Key Takeaways

Based on findings from my user interviews, my hypothesis was correct. A music compatibility feature will add a more social and interactive element to Spotify’s existing social capabilities. With the addition of a music compatibility feature, users will:

  • Feel more compelled to visit user profiles
  • Have the ability to develop new friendships with people they otherwise did not think they were compatible with
  • Help users create playlists for other users with more confidence

Based on my research overall, I believe that integrating a music compatibility feature into user profiles will make the most sense, considering users already utilize user profiles as a way to see what artists and playlists someone listens to.

Also, user profiles on Spotify are an underutilized form of real estate on Spotify’s website, especially when compared to Apple Music. By adding this feature to user profiles, there will be more traffic driven towards user profiles.

Define


POV and HMW

Using insights found in my user interviews, I created point of views (POVs) to get a sense of what the target user needs. I then generated How Might We (HMW) questions from those POVs in order to create a starting point to brainstorm possible solutions from.

Brainstorming

I sketched out ideas on Procreate to come up with solutions to the HMW questions I came up with. My goal in creating these sketches was to continuously come up with new ideas without sticking to just one solution.




Key Takeaways

Coming up with possible solutions to my HMW questions was fun but also challenging because I had to come up with different ways to visualize data--something I never really done before. Not only did I need to think about how to visualize this data, but I also needed to think about what data would be of interest to users in the first place. Because of this, usability testing will be key in determining how my data visualizations will be understood and enjoyed by everyday Spotify users.

Testing


Capturing insights on what users think about my data visualizations was important. This is why I decided to test with a high fidelity prototype so the data visualizations I created were polished and ready to be interactive.

View Testing Prototype

Usability Testing

I wanted to get feedback from a range of Spotify users so my only requirement was that all 14 participants were primarily Spotify users. To test my prototype, I decided to use Maze for the first time to see what unmoderated testing was like.

As I started to get results, I realized that unmoderated testing can come with some limitations. Some users had trouble completing tasks and were unsure how to navigate between some of the screens. I believe this is due to:

  • Usability test directions being a little unclear.
  • Participants were unfamiliar with the limited functionality of prototypes combined with the unfamiliarity of Maze’s interface.
  • Some participants, who don’t use Spotify on desktop, were already unfamiliar with how to find friends and user profiles--which was two of the big tasks being tested.

Initially, it was a little discouraging to see participants having difficulties with task completion. However, it was important for me to realize that these are still valuable insights.
These insights include:

  • The carousel to browse through the compatibility page was not intuitive for users.
  • It was not intuitive that users can interact with the data by hovering over the data visualizations.

Affinity Maps

Because I received feedback on my prototype from a range of Spotify users, I wanted to see how feedback on my prototype correlated with both general Spotify usage and usage of specifically Spotify’s social features--mainly following others.

I divided my results between two archetypes, the ‘Lone Wolf’ Spotify user and the ‘Social Butterfly’ Spotify user. I created boards describing these users using information entirely found from usability testing.







With these archetypes created, it became easier to hone in on the ‘Social Butterfly’ Spotify user who would most likely use this music compatibility feature. I determined both wins and opportunities for improvement based on feedback from this user. This way, it became easier to plan out what my next iterations of my designs will look like.






Final Iterations

I made two main revisions based on findings from the affinity map:

  • To make the carousel clearer for users to navigate, I made the carousel buttons bigger and included directions on the first compatibility page to click through the carousel buttons.
  • To make it clearer to users that the data visualizations are interactive, I included directions like "Hover over to learn more" throughout the compatibility pages.


As for general UI revisions, I also added artist photos to the bubbles data visualization and restructured the layout for all of the compatibility pages.

Next Steps

I would like to conduct moderated usability tests to see if the revisions I made help issues found in my initial usability testing sessions.

Since I focused on the desktop Spotify experience for this project, a plausible next step would be to incorporate this feature in Spotify’s mobile app experience.

Eventually, I’d like to add features brought up from participants in my user interviews as well as usability testing. These include:

  • The ability to not share your music compatibility data. Due to some privacy concerns brought up in my initial usability testing sessions, I would like to add a toggle button to give users the option to ‘turn off’ data on their listening habits being shared.
  • Genre breakdown. Some usability test participants were interested in learning more about overlapping genres as another aspect of music compatibility.
  • The ability to see your music compatibility with artists. This was a feature all of my participants in my user interviews were interested in seeing.

* * * * *

Final Thoughts


It was great to experience a very realistic project brief where I needed to figure out how to add a new, useful feature to an existing (and well-established) platform like Spotify. This project gave me the opportunity to dip my toes into the world of data visualization while navigating a deceptively difficult project brief.

It was really cool to see firsthand how data visualization and user experience design overlap. Both data visualization and UX design require effective storytelling through visual means and a strong understanding of information hierarchy. They both also require frequent communication with the target users to gain insights and clarity on how to make a design both worthwhile and delightful. I think that by seeing these parallels between data visualization and UX play out throughout this project, I have a much stronger appreciation and understanding of how the design thinking process is applicable to any field.
