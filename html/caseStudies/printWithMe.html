PrintWithMe: A Responsive Redesign


Role
UX/UX Designer
Researcher
Project Duration
4 weeks (40 hrs/wk)
UX Academy Student Project
tools
Sketch, Whimsical, Miro, Invision, Figma, OptimalSort

Overview


PrintWithMe aims to meet consumers who don’t necessarily need a printer at home but still have one-off printing needs for things like shipping and return labels.

The scope of this project is to research and redesign PrintWithMe's current website to create a stronger user experience.


The Problem

PrintWithMe offers people the convenience of printing on the go without any of the responsibilities that come with owning and maintaining a printer at home. Though PrintWithMe’s mission is to make printing both accessible and convenient, there are still opportunities to improve the end-to-end printing experience and make the process more streamlined for users.

There are three key aspects of using PrintWithMe's self-service printers that cause frustration with users. These are:

  • A homepage that has a lot of information targeting potential partners and vendors, not the everyday user who wants to use PrintWithMe’s self-service printers.
  • A difficult-to-use location search tool that relies on users dragging around a map to find locations near them.
  • An email-to-print process that makes the user go back and forth from the PrintWithMe site and their email inbox, making the printing and check out process more time-consuming.

How might we make the end-to-end printing experience with PrintWithMe more streamlined?

The Solution

  • A redesigned homepage that provides easily accessible information for first-time and returning users that want to use a self-service printer.
  • A location search tool that allows users to input their location and provides a list of location results.
  • A streamlined process for users to upload their documents directly on the site, preview their document, check out, and receive a QR code to retrieve their document.

Design Thinking Process

* * * * *

Research


My overarching research goal was to develop an in-depth understanding of the end-to-end user experience behind using a self-service printer, from uploading a document to service at home to retrieving a document at a printer location. Then I wanted to use this understanding to identify any opportunities to improve the overall user experience.

Some of my guiding research questions were:

  • What are common features that exist across different printing services? What features stick out?
  • What makes printing at a print service location an enjoyable experience?
  • Discover any opportunities to improve on the end-to-end user experience.
  • Identify the different touchpoints (website, business location, physical printer, etc) across systems for using a printer service.

Competitive Analysis

I wanted to take a look at what printing services by competitors and discover if there are any gaps or barriers that exist within those services.





I conducted a competitive analysis across three competitors: Office Depot, Staples and FedEx. I chose these companies because their self-printing services are pretty similar to that of PrintwithMe’s. I went through their websites and looked for strengths and weaknesses across the three platforms.



Then, I took a more detailed look at the different services offered across the 3 direct competitors. I included PrintWithMe to understand how they compare with their competitors. Since my goal is to redesign the end-to-end printing experience for PrintWithMe, I looked at both physical and digital aspects of the services offered.





Heuristic Evaluation

The usability of these websites plays a huge role in a user's printing experience because it's one of the main entry points to any printing service. It was important to test the usability of PrintWithMe’s site against its competitors and look for similarities, along with rooms for improvement. I tested different aspects of the usability of the websites and rated these aspects using a color scale.





Customer Interviews with Task Analysis

At this point, I gauged the strengths and weaknesses of PrintWithMe's website, and of the websites of its competitors. However, I needed to see how the usability of these websites would play out with real users. I conducted 1:1 customer interviews with 3 participants in order to observe their experiences with competitors’ websites as they completed three main tasks for each site. This way, I can test my initial assumptions of what works with PrintWithMe’s website, and what doesn’t.

The tasks participants were asked to complete for each website were:

  1. Explore homepage and tell me what your first impressions are of this website
  2. Look for information on how to use a self-service printer
  3. Look for the nearest self-service printer location near you


For these customer interviews, I asked participants to complete tasks on three websites total: Staples, FedEx, and PrintWithMe. While Office Depot was identified as a direct competitor, findings from my competitive analysis and heuristic evaluation found that Office Depot was a weaker competitor compared to Staples and FedEx. Likewise, I did not want to overload the interview participants with too many websites, especially with there being three tasks to complete for each website.




One key finding was that all participants identified PrintWithMe’s location search tool as the most difficult to use across the websites tested.
This was because:

  • The search tool only provided a dropdown selection of states and did not give users the option to input their location, either manually or automatically (with location sharing)
  • There was no list of location results and users had to drag around the map to find locations near them.
" If you’re not good with maps, it’s not helpful at all."

Some other key findings were:

  • Staples was considered amongst all participants to have the strongest location search tool. Staples’ location search tool had all the elements PrintWithMe’s location search tool did not have, with the added bonus of an attractive, easy-to-use interface.
  • Two out of the three participants were unable to find information on how to use PrintWithMe's self-service printers.
  • Two of the participants said that if they needed to print something for a one-off reason, they would normally print something at work, before the COVID pandemic. Now, amidst the COVID pandemic, they have either asked someone else to print the document from their workplace or explored printing services in their area.
  • In spite of the negative comments about PrintWithMe’s usability, there was still interest in the service. One participant says they are excited to use PrintWithMe in the future because it gives them the opportunity to explore a new place in their area, and maybe get something to drink or eat while they print their document.
" I would use PrintWithMe because it looks fun. It’s in a tea lounge so I could get tea while I’m there and maybe get something to eat."

How might make it easier for users to find information on how to use PrintWithMe's self-service printers?




Key Takeaways

After conducting research, it’s clear that there are aspects of PrintWithMe that lags behind its competitors. However, all of these aspects are completely fixable! After researching PrintWithMe’s competitors, I now have a stronger understanding of how to approach a redesign of PrintWithMe’s end-to-end user experience. Using elements from Staples and Fedex will be key to generating new ideas on how to improve PrintWithMe’s website, printer location search tool, and overall printing experience.


* * * * *

Define


Before coming up with a design to solve our problem, I needed to further define the user and understand how they would navigate the different touch points, both digital and physical, within the end-to-end printing experience.


User Persona

Based on the user interviews conducted, I created one main user persona named Marcel who personifies the findings from my customer interviews. Marcel is a 27 yr old, tech-savvy student. While he occasionally has printing needs, they are not enough to justify buying a printer.




Storyboard

Now, I wanted to get a thorough understanding of what barriers exist when printing a document with PrintWithMe. So, I created a storyboard narrating the user’s current end-to-end experience with PrintWithMe. In order to demonstrate an authentic experience, I kept the following in mind when creating this initial storyboard:

  • Aspects of this experience will be pulled from my competitive analysis and heuristic evaluation.
  • One of customer interviews uncovered a prior experience with PrintWithMe. Elements from this user’s experience are incorporated in this first storyboard.


Next, I created a storyboard showing what the user’s end-to-end experience could look like with a redesign of the experience.





User Flows

The two storyboards I created helped me get a sense of the user’s end-to-end experience when printing with PrintWithMe. This experience starts from the user’s first time navigating the PrintWithMe homepage to actually going to a printer location and printing a document. I developed a user task flow using information from the two storyboards created.

This user task flow incorporates both the online and physical steps a user takes in order to print a document and is punctuated with key decision points the user makes when printing a document.

Initial Designs


Low Fidelity Wireframes

I created low fidelity wireframes of three main pages across mobile, tablet and desktop. These main pages include: homepage, location page and confirmation page.

For my homepage wireframes, I maintained the majority of the copywriting found on PrintWithMe’s current site. I mainly restructured the homepage so there was a clearer sense of hierarchy regarding the information found on the page.


Then, I designed the location page and confirmation pages. Because these pages do not exist within PrintWithMe’s current site, I designed these pages from scratch. I incorporated elements from the Staples’ and FedEx’s location pages that users liked in my customer interviews and built on those design systems.






Then I designed screens for the user task flow of uploading a document to print.

Prototyping and Testing



Low-Fidelity Prototype

I created a low-fidelity prototype to test two user flows: searching for a printer location and uploading a document to print. I opted to create a low-fidelity prototype for testing so I could focus on testing the user’s experience with the usability and interactions of the site. Since my design solution centers on redesigning the user experience with the homepage, the location search tool and the process of uploading a document, I wanted to test only those solutions--not what users think about the visual design of the site.



View Testing Prototype

Usability Testing

For my usability testing sesions, I recruited 4 participants ages 25-30 years old that are relatively tech savvy and do not own a printer.

My objectives for usability testing were:

  • Determine if the redesigned site helps users find information faster.
  • Determine if the redesigned location search tool is easier to use.
  • Understand how users feel about the redesigned printing process and see if there are any barriers or gaps in the new printing experience.

Wins include:

  • All participants found the location search tool easy to use and said that the location search results page was their favorite page throughout the experience. This was awesome to hear since one of my main design goals was to make PrintWithMe’s existing location search tool easier to use.
  • All participants quickly found information about how to use a PrintwithMe printer on the homepage.
  • Participants liked that there were a variety of ways to upload their document, via DropBox, Google Drive, OneDrive or their device.

Opportunities for improvement include:

  • Some participants didn't realize that they had only 24 hours to go to a printer location and print their document, until they reached the confirmation page. This can be fixed by making the ‘How It Works’ section on the homepage clearer and more comprehensive.
  • Some users also were quick to leave the confirmation page, not understanding that they needed to save the QR code before leaving the page in order to retrieve their document. It needs to be clearer that users should save the QR code before leaving the confirmation page.
  • One user asked about how COVID-19 would affect certain locations since some restaurants and cafes have strict rules about the number of guests allowed inside. This can be fixed with a COVID-19 announcement banner on the location page.
  • There was feedback from users about the placement of certain buttons throughout the site. Suggestions included making the toggle button more visible in location search tool, and changing the placement of the 'continue as guest' button when checking out.

Affinity Maps

I put all comments found during testing on a Miro board then organized those comments into groupings. In doing so, it was easy to see patterns and assign priority values to still-existing problems. I developed another Miro board below to come up with both quick fixes (across design and usability issues) and long-term solutions.

Key Takeaways

With usability testing, I received a lot of user comments about what worked and didn’t work usability-wise. I learned that it's necessary to assign priority levels to user feedback, otherwise it's easy to get lost in the weeds when making Iterations. One way to do this was creating an affinity map because then I can see which areas of feedback are most common with users. Now I feel confident deciding which design elements to reiterate on when creating my high-fidelity iterations.

Final Designs


UI Kit

In my initial customer test interviews, users had positive feedback towards the branding, logo and imagery on PrintWithMe’s current site so I decided to not make any changes to those aspects of the design system. I utilized that feedback to create high-end designs, consistent with PrintWithMe’s existing branding, for handoff and delivery.




High-Fidelity Iterations

I created high fidelity wireframes using the UI Kit I created and made changes based on some key findings in my usability testing. I focused on quick fixes including copywriting issues and button placements.

Next Steps

I feel confident in the high-fidelity iteration I made for the mobile prototype though I can see there is still room for improvement in terms of nice-to-haves. These include:

  • Information on the printer location page about how many people are in line for the printer.
  • Add feature that shows printer is up and running.
  • Add option to another document at the end of the confirmation page, in case the user would like upload another document.

* * * * *

Final Thoughts


I found this project to be pretty challenging since it incorporates aspects of both service design and user experience design. When I first considered redesigning PrintWithMe, I loved the idea of providing printing services to people who cannot justify buying a printer. This is especially highlighted in the age of COVID where now people who once (covertly) used their work printers for their one-off printing needs no longer have that option. I wanted to make this service, that’s becoming even more of a need, more enjoyable for first-time and existing users. While there are aspects in my design solution that might be ambitious and not completely congruent to business needs--such as a QR code scanner, it was exciting to completely rethink the existing PrintWithMe experience and imagine the ideal experience.
