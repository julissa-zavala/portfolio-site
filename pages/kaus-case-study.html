Kaus Insurance: E-Commerce Website

Role
UX/UX Designer
Researcher
Project Duration
4 weeks (40 hrs/wk)
UX Academy Student Project
tools
Sketch, Whimsical, Miro, Invision, Figma, OptimalSort

Overview

Kaus is a large (fictitious) insurance company that has been in business for 30 years. Their goal is to transition into a B2C market and target younger consumers.

The Problem

How can we meet the business needs of Kaus Insurance while meeting the needs of the target users? Young working professionals gravitate towards sites that make purchasing insurance easy to understand. These users also tend to have a general mistrust of insurance companies. Kaus needs to examine how to instill trust and how to market their insurance as a need for this user base.

How might we make purchasing insurance easy to understand?

The Solution

A redesigned e-commerce site that makes purchasing insurance from Kaus a streamlined experience.

Design Thinking Process

* * * * *

Research

My goal was to understand the user’s decision-making process when buying insurance. I made a research plan with these overarching questions to answer:

  • What are the thought processes behind purchasing insurance?
  • What do some user's prior experiences purchasing insurance look like?
  • What are some barriers or gaps in the insurance market?
  • What develops customer loyalty towards an insurance company?

Competitive Analysis

My secondary research on the insurance market showed opportunities for disruption and innovation. For example, there is a rise in 'Insurtech’ companies turning to AI and smartphone apps for their quote tools.

I then explored the insurance market to see how existing products are meeting our target user's needs. I focused on six competitors, three direct and three indirect.

I found that many insurance companies are targeting young working professionals by:

  • focusing on values like 'transparency', 'fairness', 'simplicity' and 'affordability.'
  • having competely online presence.
  • making their smooth quote process accessible right from the homepage.

User Interviews

I held 1:1 user interviews with four research participants to learn about their motivations, needs, and pain points when purchasing insurance. All participants said that when they last bought insurance, they did not speak to an agent at any point in the process. These participants felt confident enough with the information found on the website.

Two participants currently have renters insurance from Lemonade. They said Lemonade's simplified quote tool played a huge role in their decision. Because of their positive experiences, they would buy insurance from Lemonade again.

Another key finding was that all the participants said they had no motivation to buy insurance in the first place. One purchased health insurance because their parents pressured her to. Two purchased renter’s insurance because their apartment buildings required it.

Some key quotes were:

" Thinking about insurance just stresses me out but I want to learn what it all means one day. "
" I'm okay with a completely online insurance company--it's not like I'm going to go in person anywhere during CO-VID."

Some other notable findings were:

  • 3 out of 4 participants needed to share proof of purchase with their landlord.
  • All 4 participants expressed an interest in learning insurance terminology in the future.
  • All 4 participants said they would pick the cheapest option available.
  • All 4 participants made unprompted comments about distrusting insurance companies.
  • All 4 participants said they had no interest or need to go to an insurance company’s physical location, especially in amid the COVID-19 pandemic.

How might we design an inviting insurance site users can trust?

Key Takeaways

It's clear that there is a need for a more accessible, simplified insurance purchasing process. While companies like Lemonade are doing just that, there are still gaps in the market. Further, many young working professionals are not motivated to buy insurance. There also is a general mistrust of insurance companies.

To solve this problem, I need to design an insurance site that young working professionals can not only trust, but also feel like they need. To do that, it's important to:

  • make insurance easy to understand
  • create a simplified quote process
  • make information about how insurance works accessible

Define

Before coming up with a design to solve our problem, I needed to further define the user and understand how they would navigate the site.

User Persona

Based on the user interviews conducted, I created one main user persona within Kaus' target demographic. This user is a young working professional who needs to buy insurance but might not know where to start. From here, this persona is who I will base future design decisions on. From here, we can use this persona to base future design decisions on.

Card Sorting & Site Map

My user interviews showed that users like sites where the information they need is easily accessible. Because of this, it was vital to learn how potential users think about the content on Kaus Insurance site. Thus, I conducted card sorting sessions via Optimalsort.

After I conducted an open sorting session with 5 participants that match my target user base, I used my findings to develop a site map.

User Flows

Now that the site map was finished, it was time to think through the different pathways users will take when they navigate around the site. I created user flows to explore these pathways and figure out what screens need to be made for the next exciting step: wireframes.

For these user flows I focused on two journeys based on my user persona, Elena:
1. How does a user, that knows what they want already, get a quote.
2. How can a user, that doesn't know what they want, use information found on the website to make the right insurance decision with this company.

Key Takeaways

With the Define stage, I established who the users of my website will be and what their objectives are. By defining the user and task flows based on one major persona, it became easier to create a site map to meet those requirements.

Moving forward to the Design stage, I now have a better idea of how to develop potential solutions including a streamlined quote process, and comprehensive, straightforward layout for the homepage.

Design

Sketches

I created multiple low fidelity wireframe sketches of the homepage for Kaus to explore potential layout options while keeping my user flow, task flow and site maps in mind.

Low Fidelity Wireframes

Based on these sketches, I created low fidelity responsive wireframes across mobile, tablet and desktop screens so users can access the site across devices and still have a consistent user experience. These wireframes are based on the homepage of the Kaus website.

Branding

Insights from the 1:1 user interviews demonstrated users are drawn to insurance sites that make insurance simple. With that in mind, I focused on making simplified logos that touch on one of the insurance policies Kaus offers: homeowner's insurance.

After creating a logo, I created a UI kit for Kaus Insurance. My goal was to make a site that looked inviting, simple and modern. My user interviews showed that purchasing insurance can be an anxiety-inducing process. Because of that, I wanted to incorporate design elements that put users at ease.

Testing

I created a prototype on Invision focusing on my main user flow: getting a quote.

Usability Testing

I conducted usability testing sessions to test the homepage, navigation bar and quote process. I held usability testing sessions with four participants and documented all feedback.

My usability test objectives were:

  • Observe if users quickly understand the site flow and can navigate it easily.
  • Test how easily a user can get a quote from the Kaus Homepage.
  • Determine if there are any areas of the site’s design where the user encounters difficulties when performing a task.

Affinity Maps

I organized insights from the usability testing sessions on an affinity map to establish any notable patterns. Then, I organized those patterns into categories to visualize design opportunities and potential next steps.

Final Iterations

Based on the affinity map, I made priority revisions throughout the site based on how quick the fix was and how impactful the fix would be. Some of these revisions are listed below

  • Reworking the homepage so there were more breaks in color to make the page feel less long.
  • To make the quote process clearer, I adjusted the font size of the directions and input fields. I also added numbers to the directions.
  • Based on some user comments about the support button looking "off", I changed the look and placement of the support button.
  • Some users said they weren't sure if the information collected was making a difference in the quotes they received. I added a feature so users can hover over and see why a question is being asked. I also included a loading page that appears before the quote tool lists packages. This way, users can feel like the quote tool is factoring in their information before presenting quotes.

Key Takeaways

Usability testing offered many insights. One main takeaway is that users feel a sense of ease with the website's design, layout and language. Users don't feel overwhelmed by the site and intuitively feel like the site is targeting young people like them. This was great to hear because this was a goal throughout the design process.

‍While I sought out to create a simplified quote process, it's clear that there are some aspects to improve on to make the process clearer to users. I initially thought that to create a streamlined quote process, the less steps the better. However, based on the comments I received from usability testing, the amounts of steps matters less than the structure of the overall process. In order to make this overall process clearer to users, it might be worth creating a juncture between the quote process and the checkout process. Revising this process and testing again will be key to making the checkout process clearer to new users.

Next Steps

  • Make revisions to prototype based on usability testing insights, including feature additions and design adjustments.
  • With the new changes, conduct more usability testing and gather feedback to improve the site and quote process.

Final Thoughts

This was the first real UX project I worked on and learned a lot from the experience. Beyond learning how to use tools like Sketch, Figma, and Invision, I learned how to fully actualize an idea from its inception to a working, testable prototype.

I also learned from this experience how I'd like to improve as a designer. After going through the entire design thinking process, I have a better sense of what questions to ask in that initial research stage.

After working on this project over the course of 4 weeks, I went through a crash course of the UX design process. If I had more time on this project, I would like to experience the reiterative aspect of the design process by continuing to revise my prototype and test it. Now that I know how the test stage looks like, I feel like I can develop prototypes with clear usability questions in mind.

Overall, I enjoyed this experience and look forward to working on new UX projects armed with this new knowledge and perspective.
