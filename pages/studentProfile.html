Redesigning the Student Profile



Redesigning the Student Profile

Role
Lead Product Designer
Project Duration
4 weeks
Tools
Figma, Miro, Mixpanel

* * *

Overview


What is the Portal?

The Portal serves as a school management tool accessible to all NYC public schools and over 200 housing shelters in the city. It simplifies access to student data for thousands of NYC educators through comprehensive daily updates. The aim is to equip educators with an efficient tool, eliminating the need for manual data collection and analysis.

Why redesign this page?

Expanding beyond NYC, the organization aimed to revamp the UI and UX of the Portal. Stakeholders identified the need for a refreshed design before pursuing further expansion. This redesign of the student profile followed a recent UI refresh of the entire Portal, focusing on modernization and improved aesthetics.

Before and after of the Student Profile page after the UI refresh work



Recognizing its significance through user conversations and analytics, the Student Profile emerged as a priority for further redesign beyond the initial UI refresh. As the most visited page in the Portal, the student profile provides users with a detailed view of a student's data, encompassing attendance, academics, and credits progress.


TL;DR

Collaborating with a product manager, I led a redesign targeting user pain points in the Student Profile page. The goal was to introduce a clear information hierarchy, enhancing the user's ability to quickly assess a student's progress across various content areas.

Discovery

Kickoff

At the project's start, we identified two pain points based on user research and stakeholder feedback: navigating through data-heavy panels and reducing the height of the student profile header.

With 12 data-heavy panels, it's cumbersome to go to a particular panel. Users get around this by collapsing all panels to see which panels are available, then navigating to the desired panel.



Many of our users access the Portal through their school's Chromebooks. This means that many of our users are viewing the student profile on a resolution of 1280x780. Vertical space on this page is valuable and the current page header takes up a sizeable amount of real estate.



While these issues offered valuable insights, the redesign lacked specific goals. Our next step was to refine our understanding of the "ideal" Student Profile experience and align the product team on a shared vision.


Defining Success

To set specific goals, we organized brainstorming sessions with the product management team and the Head of Design. This brainstorming session helped define long-term and short-term goals for the student profile design.


This template was used to gather ideas from members of the product team during a brainstorming session in Miro.



To understand the current state of the student profile, I interviewed product managers from different verticals to gather insights on user needs and workflows. This was crucial, given that the student profile comprised 12 panels, each developed independently by different teams, resulting in siloed knowledge about their design.

This is a sample Insights board for the Postsecondary panel in the student profile. Similar boards were made for each panel, outlining available panel information and leaving space for notes from interviews with product managers, documented using stickies.

Approach


This research shaped our understanding of the student profile's product strategy. To streamline, my product manager and I split the design work into phases. Phase 1 would address key pain points, including information overload, inability towards understanding student status, inefficient retrieval of information, and humanizing data. The goal: design with a clear hierarchy, strategic nudges, and a humanized student narrative.

Phase 1's four main design deliverables:

  • Explore a new layout.
  • Introduce navigation for easy panel access.
  • Rethink the student profile header, reducing its height.
  • Add an overview section for a quick progress summary.

Exploring a New Layout

I started by creating three blockframe options. I prioritized navigation space and addressed the user pain point of overwhelming data by considering an 'action items' section for actionable next steps. From there, I created three low-fi wireframes to share with stakeholders.

Before going straight to low-fidelity wireframes, it was helpful to start with more exploratory blockframes to play around with the layout of the page.

Stakeholder feedback favored the third option, leading to the decision to convert it into a two-column layout for future mobile responsiveness. The final design incorporated integrated action items into the navigation using notification badges.

The low-fidelity wireframe we landed on after stakeholder feedback on the page layout.

New Way to Navigate

Utilizing product analytics and insights from the product team, I designed a left-hand sidenav for the student profile. The order of panels reflects common user workflows and click rates. To enhance visual appeal and reduce text density, I incorporated icons into the navigation.

(Left) The sidenav for 6-12 schools, (Right) The sidenav for K-5 schools



Moving the History Log

To accommodate the new navigation, I relocated the history log to a new panel. Despite some pushback from the product team on relocating the history log, my product manager and I decided to proceed, aiming to collect user feedback during testing.

Simultaneously, another team was implementing a task management system for the Portal and there was a need to show tasks assigned to a student within the student profile. I collaborated with their designer, adding tasks to the new history log panel (now called Tasks and Notes) and incorporating pagination to maintain consistent panel height.


The new Tasks and Notes panel lets users switch between viewing assigned tasks and written notes for the student. Users can also toggle to Activity to see metadata related to actions taken on the student via the Portal.



Revamping the Header

To reduce the header height, I crafted mockups condensing the information into two lines. In line with discovery phase findings highlighting the need to alleviate the data-heavy nature, I introduced color to the header of this page.

The finalized student profile page header.



Adding a Student Snapshot

In refining the student profile for better information hierarchy and discoverability, a central goal was to create a narrative for the student and ensure users quickly comprehend how the student is performing across various content areas. Insights from product manager interviews guided the content for a new student snapshot panel at the page's top. This panel provides a summarized overview of the student's data across major content areas, allowing users to click for deeper exploration.


The student snapshot panel for High School (left), Middle School (center) and Elementary School (right).

* * *

User Insights


I assembled the various elements of the student profile design and created a prototype for user testing, which included two versions. In one version, users could click into the panel as a static page, introducing a new flow. The other version allowed seamless scrolling from panel to panel, maintaining the existing flow. Until this point, feedback primarily came from stakeholders during weekly check-ins, making user opinions on this specific navigation choice invaluable.

The feedback was unanimously positive, with only minor design adjustments needed.


Key insights Include:

  • Participants highlighted the significance of scrolling between panels, particularly for tasks like adding a graduation plan. Maintaining this scrolling capability was deemed essential.
  • I received a lot of feedback from participants about the information available in the student header, which helped define with metrics were crucial.
  • Surprisingly, participants found the new history log/notes panel more discoverable, with some perceiving it as a new and exciting feature. This positive response confirmed the validity of relocating the history log to a new panel.

* * *

Final Iterations

Based on user feedback, only minor iterations needed to be make on the metrics found in the header. Maintaining scrolling from panel to panel, however, raised questions about the interaction with the existing collapsing and expanding panel experience and the navigation bar.


Thinking Through User Flows

Introducing the navigation sidebar prompted a reevaluation of its interaction with the collapsing and expanding panel experience.


I outlined the desired behavior when users click on a nav item for a panel that is already collapsed vs scrolling to an already collapsed panel.


Additionally, streamlining the workflow for users to navigate between user profiles and complete tasks for multiple students revealed inconsistencies in panel states and default visibility. I designed diagrams to address these flows in collaboration with engineers for backend implementation.


Since users frequently flip through student profiles to complete tasks for multiple students, I outlined what the saved state of the panels should be.

Impact


It's still the early days of the launch of this redesign, but we've received overwhelmingly positive feedback from users. There also has been a noticeable uptick in user traffic to the student profile and a corresponding increase in time spent on the page since the launch of the updated student profile. Since the launch, the average of individual student profile views per user have gone up 100%.

* * *

Takeaways


In four weeks, we undertook a significant overhaul of the Student Profile in the Portal to address usability challenges systematically. Handling a tight deadline and numerous redesign possibilities, I realized how crucial it is to take a phased approach when tackling a project this large. Collaborating closely with stakeholders and seamlessly incorporating features from other teams made it a collective effort. The revamped Student Profile isn't just updated; it's now user-centric, polished, and adaptable.
